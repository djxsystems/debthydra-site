## Priority 1: Upgrade Existing Guides (High ROI, Fast)

These guides are already indexed but are missing specific mechanics that competitors lack too — first to add them wins.

### 1. minimum-payment-trap.mdx
**Gap:** No competitor explains the shrinking minimum mechanic (as your balance falls, so does your minimum, meaning you pay less each month and the timeline extends even further). No competitor shows the penalty APR (29.99%) scenario.
**Add:**
- Explicit section on how the minimum itself decreases — you're not making a steady $130/month, you're making $130 then $127 then $124 as the balance slowly drops
- A second table row showing the penalty APR scenario ($5k at 29.99% instead of 24%)
- Note that one missed payment is often enough to trigger penalty APR

### 2. what-is-debt-to-income-ratio.mdx
**Gap:** 100% of top-ranking DTI content is mortgage-framed. The renter use case (landlords use DTI ≤ 35%), personal loan use case (lenders use back-end DTI), and freelancer income calculation are completely uncovered.
**Add:**
- Section: DTI for renters (landlords typically want ≤ 35–40% including proposed rent)
- Section: DTI for personal loans and auto loans (different thresholds than mortgage)
- Section: How lenders calculate DTI for variable/freelance income (2-year average, why gaps hurt)

### 3. what-happens-if-you-dont-pay-credit-card-debt.mdx
**Gap:** Already the best guide in category. Add zombie debt mechanics and a state SOL table.
**Add:**
- Section: "Zombie debt" — how collectors buy charged-off debt for 4–7 cents and how to protect yourself
- State SOL table (15 most populous states at minimum, with disclaimer to verify)
- Note on partial payment restarting the SOL clock

## Priority 2: New Guides (Uncontested Keywords)

### 4. What to do when you can't afford more than the minimum payment
**Slug:** `cant-afford-minimum-payment`
**Target keyword:** "can't afford minimum credit card payment" / "only making minimum payments"
**Why it ranks:** Zero strong content starts from genuine financial constraint. Every guide assumes extra money exists.
**Content structure:**
- Open with income-to-debt reality check (if debt is >50% of annual gross, standard advice doesn't apply)
- Hardship programs: how to call, what to say, what to expect
- How DMPs work and which nonprofits are legitimate (NFCC-affiliated agencies)
- When to triage: which debts to protect (utilities, rent, car) vs. which to negotiate
- When Chapter 7 is worth seriously considering
- The 1099-C tax trap if debt is forgiven
**Calculation angle:** Show: on $35,000/year income ($2,917/month take-home), how much is available for debt after essential expenses?

### 5. The real cost of making minimum payments at different APRs
**Slug:** `minimum-payment-cost-by-apr`
**Target keyword:** "minimum payment credit card how long to pay off" / "credit card minimum payment calculator"
**Why it ranks:** Every competitor uses one example at one APR. Nobody shows the full matrix.
**Content structure:**
- Intro: APR matters enormously — not all 24% balances are the same as 29.99%
- Full table: 3 balance amounts ($3k, $5k, $10k) × 3 APRs (18%, 24%, 29.99%) × 3 payment levels
- Show the shrinking minimum over time (specific month-by-month for the first 12 months)
- How one missed payment can trigger penalty APR (common path to 29.99%)
- The link to the snowball/avalanche calculators for "what if I added $X/month"
**Calculation angle:** The data table IS the content.

### 6. Should I stop my 401(k) contributions to pay off debt?
**Slug:** `401k-vs-debt-payoff`
**Target keyword:** "pay off debt or invest in 401k" / "stop 401k to pay off debt"
**Why it ranks:** Every result is either "always get the match" (too simple) or "it depends" (useless). Nobody does the actual math across scenarios.
**Content structure:**
- The decision matrix: employer match rate × debt APR
- If 0% match: at what APR does stopping contributions make sense?
- If 50% match (e.g., 50 cents per dollar up to 6%): match = guaranteed 50% return; only debts at >50% APR would beat it. No credit card debt does.
- If 100% match: almost never stop contributing
- Tax angle: traditional 401k reduces taxable income, so effective APR comparison changes
- Age-specific: stopping at 45 vs. 30 has very different compound cost
- The one exception: if debt is causing genuine crisis-level stress and the minimum math is unsustainable
**Calculation angle:** Show a worked example: $5,000 at 24% APR vs. stopping $200/month contribution with 100% match.

### 8. How to pay off debt when your income is irregular
**Slug:** `pay-off-debt-variable-income`
**Target keyword:** "pay off debt variable income" / "debt payoff freelancer" / "debt when self employed"
**Why it ranks:** Every payoff framework assumes a consistent paycheck. Gig workers, freelancers, commission salespeople — a huge audience — have no content that works for them.
**Content structure:**
- The income floor concept: use your worst-month income for minimums; sweep everything above to debt on the last day of the month
- Building a one-month income buffer before aggressive payoff
- How lenders calculate DTI for variable earners (2-year average, 1099s, gaps)
- Emergency fund sizing for irregular income (3 months is for stable jobs; variable earners need 4–6 months minimum)
- Tax planning for self-employed with high-interest debt (quarterly estimates, avoiding underpayment penalty while paying down debt)
**Calculation angle:** Show two scenarios: person pays consistent $300/month vs. person sweeps variable surplus — which pays off faster?

### 9. What is zombie debt and how collectors use it
**Slug:** `zombie-debt-old-debt-collectors`
**Target keyword:** "zombie debt" / "old debt collectors calling" / "debt statute of limitations"
**Why it ranks:** SOL tables exist everywhere. Practical guidance on what to do when collectors call about old debt doesn't.
**Content structure:**
- What zombie debt is: charged-off debt bought by collectors for pennies, often resold multiple times
- How to check if a debt is time-barred (request date of last payment in writing)
- What to say and not say on the phone (never acknowledge the debt, never promise payment)
- Sending a debt validation letter (include template)
- The partial payment trap: making a $1 payment restarts the SOL clock in most states
- The credit report clock is separate from the SOL clock (7 years from first delinquency regardless)
- 50-state SOL table
**Calculation angle:** "Collector bought your $5,000 debt for $250. They make 20x their money if you pay in full. This is why they're persistent."

### 10. Credit card debt forgiveness: what actually works vs. what's a scam
**Slug:** `credit-card-debt-forgiveness`
**Target keyword:** "credit card debt forgiveness" / "debt settlement vs bankruptcy"
**Note:** High competition from debt settlement companies. Target long-tail variants: "negotiate credit card debt yourself", "1099-C after debt settlement", "debt settlement tax consequences".
**Content structure:**
- Explain the scam landscape: for-profit debt settlement companies charge 15–25% of enrolled debt while you stop paying (damaging credit intentionally) and cannot guarantee results
- What actually works: self-negotiated settlement (requires lump sum at 120+ days delinquent), NFCC nonprofit DMP (preserves accounts, ~48 months), Chapter 7 (full discharge, 7–10 year report impact), Chapter 13 (repayment plan)
- The 1099-C trap: forgiven debt is taxable income unless you're insolvent — explain the IRS insolvency exception
- Decision framework: which to choose based on debt amount, income, assets
**Calculation angle:** Show the full cost of each path for a $15,000 debt scenario.

## What Makes DebtHydra Content Win

Based on analysis of top-ranking personal finance content:

1. **Start in the middle.** Open on the scenario/problem, not the definition.
2. **Tables are the payload.** Put the data table early — it's the answer, not decoration.
3. **Name the thing readers are afraid to say.** "If you're thinking about just stopping" is a better opening than "best debt payoff strategies."
4. **Income-relative math.** "$300/month extra" means nothing without "that's 10% of take-home on a $40k salary."
5. **Decision points, not just information.** "If X, do Y. If not X, do Z."
6. **Write for the person who's already failed once.** Acknowledge the cycle.
7. **Use current data as anchor.** Average APR 22.30% (Q4 2025 Federal Reserve). Average household card balance $11,507 (NerdWallet 2025). 73% of credit card debt is from covering essentials (GetOutOfDebt.org 2026).
8. **End on one concrete action.** Not a summary. Not a motivational paragraph.

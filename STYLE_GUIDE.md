# DebtHydra Content Style Guide

This guide exists because AI-generated writing has a recognizable fingerprint, and readers notice it even when they can't name it. The goal here is content that reads like a competent person wrote it — not a chatbot trying to sound thorough.

---

## The core problem with AI writing

AI text optimizes for *seeming* complete and helpful. It hedges constantly, summarizes itself repeatedly, and wraps every section in a bow. Real writing doesn't do this. Real writing trusts the reader.

The patterns below are what to watch for and remove.

---

## Patterns to eliminate

### 1. Hedging stacks

AI loves to pile up qualifiers and softeners, especially when introducing a point that might be slightly controversial.

**Bad:**
> While every situation is different, and it's important to consider your own circumstances, there are some general principles that many financial experts suggest may be helpful for most people.

**Better:**
> The right answer depends on your interest rates and how much extra cash you have each month.

One qualifier, maximum. The reader knows their situation is personal. Don't tell them.

---

### 2. Throat-clearing transitions

These are sentences that announce what's coming instead of just saying it.

**Bad:**
> Now that we've covered what debt consolidation is, let's look at when it actually helps.

**Better:**
> Just cut to the next section.

Transitions like "Let's dive in," "Now that you know X," "With that in mind," and "Before we get into X, it's worth noting Y" are all throat-clearing. Delete them.

---

### 3. Meta-commentary on advice

Narrating your own advice is an AI tell. Just give the advice.

**Bad:**
> That script works because it does three things: states the problem clearly, shows you're trying to deal with it, and asks specifically for available options.

**Better:**
> Just cut the meta-commentary. The reader can see what the script does.

If you feel the need to explain why your example is good, the example probably isn't good enough. Fix the example.

---

### 4. Symmetrical mirror structures

AI lists often have exactly-matched pro/con pairs where each pro has a corresponding con. Real analysis is lopsided because reality is lopsided.

**Bad:**
> Pros: lower interest, simplified payments, clear timeline.
> Cons: behavioral relapse risk, fees, longer terms.

**Better:**
> The interest savings are the main reason to do this, but the behavioral risk is bigger than most people admit. More people consolidate and then run the cards back up than people in finance like to say out loud.

Lead with the thing that actually matters most, not a balanced list.

---

### 5. The "This is why" construction

This is AI's favorite way to explain things it just explained.

**Bad:**
> This is why the snowball method works for so many people.
> This is why balances feel sticky even when you're making payments.
> This is why timing matters.

Better to just not restate the point you just made. If the explanation was good, the reader understands. If it wasn't, restate it differently — don't just announce that the thing you said was important.

---

### 6. Conclusion padding

AI conclusions typically follow this formula:
1. Restate the article's premise
2. Add "You don't have to be perfect"
3. Add a call-to-action disguised as encouragement
4. Close with a vague statement about the bigger picture

**Bad:**
> Paying off debt and building savings aren't opposites. They're both moving you toward the same place: a life where money stress takes up a lot less of your mental energy.

That's not an insight. It's a placeholder. Either end on something specific and useful, or just end the article.

**Bad:**
> If you are the kind of person who needs visible progress to stay engaged, that is not weakness. That is useful self-knowledge.

This is reassurance theater. It sounds warm but says nothing actionable. Cut it.

**Better:** End on the most useful, specific, concrete thing you have to say. Then stop.

---

### 7. List-ification of everything

AI turns everything into bullet points because bullets look like content. Some things belong in lists; most things don't.

Use bullets for:
- Actual enumerable items (e.g., fees you might be charged, questions to ask)
- Steps in a process
- Quick comparisons

Don't use bullets for:
- Ideas that belong in sentences
- Anything where the items have natural connective tissue ("first," "then," "because")
- Conclusions

---

### 8. Vague value claims

**Bad:**
> Understanding how credit card interest is calculated can be an important part of managing your finances effectively.

That sentence says nothing. Everyone knows this. Delete.

**Better:** Start with the thing that actually surprises people. "If you carry a $5,000 balance at 24% APR, you're paying about $100/month in interest and making almost no progress on the principal."

## What good DebtHydra writing looks like

### Be specific with numbers

Generic: "credit card interest adds up quickly."
Better: "A $5,000 balance at 24% APR costs about $100/month in interest. A $140 minimum payment puts only $40 toward the actual balance."

Real numbers make abstract claims land. If you're writing about debt, there's almost always a calculation you can show.

### Start in the middle

Don't introduce the topic before you start the topic. Open on the thing that matters, not on a description of what you're about to explain.

**Bad opening:** "Debt consolidation is a popular strategy that many people use to simplify their payments and potentially save money on interest."

**Better opening:** "Debt consolidation has one real risk that most articles undersell: you consolidate, feel relief, and then run the cards back up. Now you have the consolidation loan *and* fresh card debt."

### Trust the reader

Don't explain the obvious. Don't add "of course, everyone's situation is different" every time you make a general statement. Don't hedge claims that aren't actually controversial.

### Write in the second person, but sparingly

"You" is good. "People" and "many individuals" are worse. But don't over-use "you" as a way to avoid making direct claims. "Paying minimums costs you more in the long run" is fine. You don't need to add "depending on your situation."

### Earn the call-to-action

Link to calculators when the calculation actually demonstrates something. "Plug your numbers in and see the total interest" works when the total interest is the point of the article. It doesn't work as a generic close on every page.

## Voice

- Direct, not brusque
- Skeptical of financial advice industry jargon ("wealth-building journey," "financial freedom," "take control of your finances")
- Comfortable with uncertainty: "this depends on your situation" is fine once; three times is avoidance
- Willing to say something is bad or risky without immediately softening it
- Numbers-forward: show the math when the math is the point

---

## Common phrases to avoid

| Avoid | Because |
|-------|---------|
| "It's important to note that..." | Just say the thing |
| "Let's dive in" | Throat-clearing |
| "The bottom line is..." | You're about to repeat yourself |
| "At the end of the day..." | Cliché |
| "Ultimately..." | Filler |
| "Financial freedom" | Jargon |
| "Take control of your finances" | Vague motivational filler |
| "It can be overwhelming..." | Assumed emotion |
| "The good news is..." | AI warmth theater |
| "The key takeaway here is..." | Just say the takeaway |
| "This is why X matters" | Restatement masquerading as insight |

---

## Article structure

No required template, but a few principles:

1. **Open on the tension or the surprising fact** — not on a definition
2. **Use H2s for genuine section breaks** — not every other paragraph
3. **Put the most useful information early** — don't make the reader scroll for it
4. **End on something specific** — not a motivational paragraph
5. **Tables and numbers beat vague claims** — if there's a calculation that proves your point, show it

---

## The test

Read the draft aloud. If any sentence sounds like something an AI assistant would say to seem helpful, cut it or rewrite it. The reader came for specific, accurate information. Give them that, then stop.

If you've started looking into paying off debt, you've probably already heard about these two methods. They're everywhere. And there's a reason for that — they both actually work. The real question isn't which one is "better." It's which one *you'll finish*.

## What is the Debt Snowball?

The snowball method, popularised by Dave Ramsey, is straightforward: **pay off your smallest balance first**, no matter the interest rate. You make minimum payments on everything else and throw all your extra cash at the smallest debt. When that one's gone, you roll that payment into the next-smallest, and so on.

Here's what that might look like:

- Credit Card A: $800 balance → pay off first
- Credit Card B: $2,200 balance → pay off second
- Car Loan: $6,500 balance → pay off last

The snowball builds momentum. Every time you clear a debt, you get a win. For a lot of people, those wins are the fuel that keeps them going.

## What is the Debt Avalanche?

The avalanche method is the mathematically optimal strategy: **pay off the highest interest rate first**, regardless of balance. The logic is simple — the highest-rate debt is costing you the most money every month. Eliminate it first and you save the most overall.

Same debts, reordered by rate:

- Credit Card B: 24.99% APR → pay off first
- Credit Card A: 19.99% APR → pay off second
- Car Loan: 7.5% APR → pay off last

If you run both through a calculator, the avalanche will almost always show a lower total interest cost.

## Which One Saves More Money?

Avalanche. Almost without exception. If you have a $20,000 debt load across several accounts, the difference can easily be $500–$2,000 in total interest, sometimes more.

But here's the catch: the avalanche might not pay off any single debt for months, or even a year, if your highest-rate debt is also a large balance. That can feel demoralising.

## Which One Should You Choose?

Ask yourself this honestly: **do you need quick wins to stay motivated?**

If yes — and there's no shame in that, most people do — go with the snowball. The "mathematically suboptimal" method that you finish is infinitely better than the optimal one you abandon after three months.

If you're the kind of person who can stare at a spreadsheet, trust the numbers, and stay disciplined even when progress feels slow, the avalanche will save you money.

Some people even do a hybrid: they tackle one small debt with the snowball just to get a win, then switch to avalanche. Do whatever works for you.

## A Few Things Neither Method Accounts For

- **Variable interest rates**: Credit card rates can change. Rerun your plan periodically.
- **Balance transfers**: A 0% intro APR offer can dramatically change your payoff math.
- **Life happens**: Job loss, medical bills, car repairs — any of these can disrupt a plan. Having a small emergency fund alongside your debt payoff is worth it.

## Try Both Calculators

The best way to decide is to run your actual numbers through both and compare. See how many months each takes, and how much interest you'd save with the avalanche. That gap is the cost of the snowball's motivational benefit — you get to decide if it's worth it.
